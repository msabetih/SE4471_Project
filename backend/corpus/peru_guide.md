# Peru Travel Guide

## Overview
Peru is one of South America's most extraordinary travel destinations, home to Machu Picchu (one of the New Seven Wonders of the World), the Amazon rainforest, Lake Titicaca, and the vibrant colonial city of Cusco. Its dramatic geography ranges from Pacific coastline to Andean peaks over 6,000m to lowland jungle — often within hours of each other.

## Top Destinations

### Machu Picchu
The 15th-century Inca citadel set high in the Andes is Peru's most iconic sight. Entrance tickets must be purchased in advance online through the official Peruvian government portal — capacity is limited and tickets sell out weeks ahead, especially for Sun Gate (Inti Punku) and Huayna Picchu mountain hikes. Getting there: most visitors take the train from Cusco or Ollantaytambo to Aguas Calientes (the town at the base), then a bus up the mountain. Alternatively, hike the Inca Trail (4-day, book 6+ months in advance) or Salkantay Trek (5-day, no permit required). Go early morning to beat crowds and clouds.

### Cusco
The former capital of the Inca Empire at 3,400m altitude — allow 1–2 days to acclimatize before any strenuous activity. The Plaza de Armas (main square), Qorikancha (Temple of the Sun), and the nearby Sacsayhuamán fortress are key sites. San Blas neighborhood has artisan workshops. The boleto turístico (tourist pass) covers most sites. Cusco has a vibrant restaurant and bar scene — Chicha por Gastón Acurio and Cicciolina are standout restaurants.

### The Sacred Valley
The valley between Cusco and Machu Picchu has Inca ruins at every turn: Ollantaytambo (a living Inca town with steep terraces), Pisac (hilltop fortress and Sunday market), and Moray (mysterious circular agricultural terracing). The valley's lower altitude (2,800m) makes it a more comfortable acclimatization base than Cusco. Salt pans (Salineras de Maras) fed by a natural saltwater spring have been harvested since Inca times.

### Lake Titicaca (Puno)
At 3,812m, the world's highest navigable lake straddles Peru and Bolivia. The floating reed islands of Uros are the most famous attraction — indigenous communities live on man-made islands. Taquile Island has an extraordinary weaving tradition (UNESCO Intangible Heritage). Puno itself is basic but the gateway. Overnight stays on islands are possible for a more authentic experience.

### Lima
Peru's coastal capital is a culinary powerhouse — Lima has more entries on Latin America's 50 Best Restaurants list than any other city. Miraflores and Barranco are the upscale oceanfront districts with excellent restaurants (Central, Maido, Huaca Pucllana). The Larco Museum has the finest collection of pre-Columbian art in the Americas (and a famous erotic pottery collection). The historic center (Centro Histórico) is a UNESCO World Heritage site with baroque churches.

### The Amazon (Iquitos or Puerto Maldonado)
Iquitos is the world's largest city inaccessible by road — reached only by plane or multi-day riverboat. The lodges along the Amazon tributaries offer wildlife (pink dolphins, caimans, sloths, macaws, piranhas) and guided jungle walks. Puerto Maldonado (accessible by air from Cusco, or overland) is the gateway to the Tambopata and Madre de Dios reserves — easier logistics, still outstanding wildlife.

## Transportation
- **Domestic flights:** LATAM Peru, Sky Airline, and Star Perú connect Lima to Cusco (1.5 hours), Iquitos, Puno, and other cities. The road to Cusco is very long — fly unless you're doing an overland adventure.
- **Trains:** Peru Rail and Inca Rail connect Cusco/Ollantaytambo to Aguas Calientes (Machu Picchu gateway). Book well in advance.
- **Inca Trail & trekking permits:** Book 6+ months ahead for the classic Inca Trail (limited to 500 people/day including porters).
- **Taxis / colectivos:** Negotiate prices before getting in taxis. Use Uber or Cabify in Lima.

## Budget Estimates (per day)
- Budget: $30–50 CAD — hostels, market meals (menú del día for $3–5), colectivos, student discounts at sites
- Moderate: $80–130 CAD — mid-range hotels, restaurant meals, train tickets, guided tours
- Luxury: $250+ CAD — luxury train (Belmond Hiram Bingham), Inkaterra or Belmond lodges, private guides

## Best Time to Visit
- **May–September (dry season):** Best for Machu Picchu, trekking, and the Sacred Valley. Clear skies, but cold nights at altitude.
- **October–April (wet season):** Lush green landscapes; Machu Picchu can have mist and rain. The Amazon is best November–April when water levels are high for wildlife spotting.
- **June:** Inti Raymi (Festival of the Sun) in Cusco (June 24) — spectacular but crowded.

## Altitude Sickness (Soroche)
Flying directly to Cusco (3,400m) often causes altitude sickness: headache, nausea, fatigue. Take it easy for 24–48 hours. Avoid alcohol on day 1. Coca tea (freely available and legal) helps. Diamox (acetazolamide) can be taken prophylactically — consult a doctor before travel. Acclimatize in the Sacred Valley first if possible.

## Visa & Entry
Citizens of the USA, Canada, UK, EU, and Australia do not need a visa to enter Peru for tourism (up to 90 days). A valid passport and proof of onward travel are required. No vaccinations are officially required, but yellow fever vaccination is recommended if visiting the Amazon.
