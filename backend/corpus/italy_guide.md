# Italy Travel Guide

## Overview
Italy is a living museum — a country where ancient Roman ruins sit beside Renaissance masterpieces, and where every region has its own dialect, cuisine, and character. From the canals of Venice and the art cities of Florence and Rome to the Amalfi Coast and the volcanic islands of Sicily, Italy rewards slow, immersive travel.

## Top Destinations

### Rome
The Eternal City layers 3,000 years of history. Essential visits: the Colosseum and Roman Forum (book timed-entry tickets online), the Vatican Museums and Sistine Chapel (reserve well in advance), St. Peter's Basilica, the Trevi Fountain, and the Pantheon. Trastevere is the best neighborhood for evening dining and atmosphere.

### Florence
The cradle of the Renaissance. The Uffizi Gallery houses Botticelli's "Birth of Venus" and Michelangelo's "Primavera" — reserve tickets months ahead in high season. Michelangelo's David is at the Accademia Gallery. The Duomo (Santa Maria del Fiore) and its climb to the top of the cupola offer panoramic views. The Oltrarno neighborhood across the Arno has artisan workshops and fewer tourists.

### Venice
Built on 118 islands connected by 400+ bridges. St. Mark's Square (Piazza San Marco) and the Basilica are the main landmarks. The Rialto Bridge and Market, the Doge's Palace, and a vaporetto (water bus) ride down the Grand Canal are essential. For a quieter experience, explore the islands of Murano (glass-blowing), Burano (colorful fishermen's houses), and Torcello.

### The Amalfi Coast
A UNESCO-listed 50km stretch of dramatic cliffs, pastel-colored fishing villages, and turquoise water. Positano is the most photographed village. Ravello has a stunning clifftop villa and gardens. Amalfi town has a beautiful Arab-Norman cathedral. The road is narrow and winding — ferries between towns are often more pleasant than driving.

### Sicily
Italy's largest island has its own distinct culture blending Greek, Arab, Norman, and Spanish influences. Palermo's street markets (Ballarò, Vucciria) and baroque churches are unmissable. The Valley of the Temples in Agrigento is one of the finest examples of ancient Greek architecture outside Greece. Mount Etna, Europe's largest active volcano, can be hiked.

## Transportation
- **Trenitalia / Italo:** High-speed trains (Frecciarossa) connect Rome, Florence, Venice, Milan, and Naples in 1.5–3 hours. Book online in advance for best prices.
- **Regional trains:** Cheaper and slower but reach smaller towns. No reservation needed.
- **Ferries:** Essential for islands (Sicily, Sardinia, Capri, Aeolian Islands) and along the Amalfi Coast.
- **Car rental:** Best for exploring rural Tuscany, Umbria, and Sicily. Avoid driving in city centers (ZTL restricted zones with automatic fines).

## Budget Estimates (per day)
- Budget: €50–70 — hostels, pizza al taglio (by the slice), aperitivo happy hours with free snacks
- Moderate: €120–160 — 3-star hotels or B&Bs, trattoria meals, major museum admissions
- Luxury: €250+ — boutique hotels, fine dining, private guided tours

## Best Time to Visit
- **April–June:** Ideal — warm weather, spring flowers, manageable crowds.
- **September–October:** Excellent — summer heat subsides, grape harvest season in Tuscany and Piedmont.
- **July–August:** Very hot in the south (35–40°C), extremely crowded and expensive in Venice and the Amalfi Coast.
- **November–March:** Fewer tourists, lower prices. Some coastal areas close. Rome and Florence are pleasant in winter.

## Visa & Entry
EU citizens travel freely. Visitors from the USA, Canada, UK, Australia, and most Western countries can stay visa-free for up to 90 days in the Schengen Area. An ETIAS authorization will be required starting 2025.

## Cultural Tips
- Cappuccino is a breakfast drink — ordering one after a meal is a tourist giveaway.
- Cover your shoulders and knees when entering churches (a scarf works).
- "Coperto" on your restaurant bill is a cover charge (€1–3) — it's normal and not optional.
- Validate your train ticket before boarding regional trains or face a fine.
- Gelato from a gelateria with covered display (not piled high in bright colors) is usually higher quality.
