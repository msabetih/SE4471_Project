# Thailand Travel Guide

## Overview
Thailand is one of Southeast Asia's most popular travel destinations, offering an extraordinary blend of ornate temples, tropical beaches, vibrant street food culture, and warm hospitality. It is accessible, affordable, and suitable for all types of travelers — from backpackers to luxury resort guests.

## Top Destinations

### Bangkok
Thailand's capital is a sensory overload in the best way. The Grand Palace and Wat Phra Kaew (Temple of the Emerald Buddha) are the city's most iconic landmarks — dress modestly to enter. Wat Pho houses a massive reclining Buddha and is the traditional home of Thai massage. The Chatuchak Weekend Market is one of the world's largest markets with 15,000+ stalls. Khao San Road is the backpacker hub; Sukhumvit is more upscale. Rooftop bars offer stunning skyline views.

### Chiang Mai
Northern Thailand's cultural capital is surrounded by mountains and jungle. The old city (moat-enclosed) has hundreds of temples including Wat Chedi Luang and Wat Phra Singh. The Sunday Night Market on Wualai Road is famous for handicrafts. Nearby: Doi Inthanon National Park (Thailand's highest peak), elephant sanctuaries (choose ethical ones that don't offer rides), and day trips to hill tribe villages.

### The Islands

**Koh Samui:** Popular resort island with developed infrastructure. Chaweng Beach is the liveliest; Maenam and Bophut are quieter. Ang Thong Marine National Park day trip is highly recommended.

**Koh Phangan:** Known for the Full Moon Party (monthly) but also has beautiful quiet beaches. Bottle Beach and Haad Salad are peaceful alternatives.

**Koh Tao:** Small island, world-class diving and snorkeling. Great for PADI open water courses at affordable prices.

**Krabi & Railay Beach:** Dramatic limestone karst cliffs, accessible only by longtail boat. Excellent rock climbing and island-hopping. Ao Nang is the main town.

**Phuket:** Thailand's largest island and most developed resort destination. Patong is the busiest. Rawai and Kata are calmer. The Similan Islands (day trip) offer some of Asia's best diving.

### Ayutthaya
Thailand's former capital (1350–1767), just 1.5 hours from Bangkok by train. A UNESCO World Heritage site of ruined temples and Buddha statues. Best explored by bicycle or tuk-tuk.

## Transportation
- **BTS Skytrain / MRT (Bangkok):** Clean and efficient. A Rabbit card or MRT card saves on single fares.
- **Overnight trains:** The Bangkok–Chiang Mai sleeper train is a classic, scenic experience (12 hours, book in advance).
- **Domestic flights:** AirAsia, Nok Air, and Bangkok Airways connect Bangkok to islands and northern cities cheaply (book early).
- **Ferries:** Multiple ferry companies serve the islands. Lomprayah and Seatran are reliable operators.
- **Grab app:** Southeast Asia's ride-hailing app — more reliable and safer than negotiating with tuk-tuks for longer distances.

## Budget Estimates (per day)
- Budget: 600–1,200 THB (~$17–35 USD) — guesthouses, street food, local transport, free temples
- Moderate: 1,500–3,000 THB (~$43–85 USD) — mid-range hotels, restaurant meals, island ferry tickets
- Luxury: 5,000+ THB (~$140+ USD) — beachfront resorts, private tours, spa treatments

## Best Time to Visit
- **November–April (cool/dry season):** Best overall. Gulf Coast islands (Koh Samui, Koh Phangan) are best November–March. Andaman Coast (Phuket, Krabi) best December–April.
- **May–October (monsoon season):** Heavy rain, especially on Andaman Coast. Koh Samui has its own rain season October–December. Prices are lower and crowds thinner.
- Chiang Mai: November–February is ideal (cool, dry). March–April sees heavy smoke from agricultural burning.

## Visa & Entry
Citizens of about 60 countries (including USA, UK, EU, Canada, Australia) receive a 30-day visa exemption stamp on arrival. Thailand also offers a 60-day tourist visa obtainable at Thai embassies. A Thailand e-Visa is available online. Passport must be valid for at least 6 months.

## Cultural Tips
- The monarchy is deeply revered — never make disrespectful comments about the royal family (lèse-majesté laws apply).
- Remove shoes before entering temples and homes.
- The "wai" (pressing palms together and bowing slightly) is the traditional greeting — lower head more deeply for elders.
- Dress modestly at temples: cover shoulders and knees. Sarongs are often available to borrow at major sites.
- Bargaining is acceptable at markets but not in malls or restaurants.
- Tap water is not safe to drink — buy bottled water or use a filtered water bottle.
