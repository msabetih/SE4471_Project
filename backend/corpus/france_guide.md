# France Travel Guide

## Overview
France is the world's most visited country, drawing tens of millions of tourists annually. From the romantic streets of Paris to the lavender fields of Provence, the vineyards of Bordeaux, and the glamour of the French Riviera, France offers extraordinary diversity across culture, cuisine, and landscape.

## Top Destinations

### Paris
The City of Light needs little introduction. Essential sites include the Eiffel Tower (book tickets online in advance), the Louvre Museum (home to the Mona Lisa — arrive early), Notre-Dame Cathedral (under restoration but exterior accessible), Montmartre and Sacré-Cœur, and the Champs-Élysées. The Marais district is excellent for galleries, falafel, and boutiques. Paris has 20 arrondissements (districts) — staying in the 1st–7th puts you closest to major sites.

### The French Riviera (Côte d'Azur)
Nice, Cannes, and Monaco line this glamorous Mediterranean coastline. Nice's Promenade des Anglais, the old town (Vieux-Nice), and the Cours Saleya flower market are highlights. Monaco is a short train ride and offers the famous casino and Formula 1 circuit streets. Antibes and Èze village are quieter alternatives.

### Provence
Lavender blooms from late June through early August in fields around Valensole and Sault. The Luberon region has stunning hilltop villages: Gordes, Roussillon (ochre cliffs), and Les Baux-de-Provence. Avignon hosts the Palais des Papes, a UNESCO World Heritage site.

### Loire Valley
Known as the "Garden of France," this UNESCO-listed valley contains over 300 châteaux. Chambord, Chenonceau (spanning the Cher River), and Amboise are the most famous. Ideal as a cycling trip or day trips from Tours or Blois.

### Bordeaux & Wine Regions
Bordeaux is a UNESCO World Heritage city with beautiful 18th-century architecture. The surrounding wine regions — Médoc, Saint-Émilion, Pomerol — offer world-class wine tours and tastings. Burgundy (around Dijon) and Champagne (around Reims) are also essential for wine lovers.

## Transportation
- **TGV (high-speed train):** Connects Paris to major cities in 1–3 hours. Book via SNCF (sncf-connect.com) — advance purchase saves significantly.
- **Paris Metro:** 16 lines covering the city thoroughly. A carnet (book of 10 tickets) or Navigo weekly pass are cost-effective.
- **Car rental:** Essential for exploring rural Provence, Loire Valley, and wine country at your own pace.

## Budget Estimates (per day)
- Budget: €60–80 — hostels, baguette sandwiches, picnics with market produce, free museums on first Sunday of the month
- Moderate: €120–180 — 3-star hotels, bistro lunches (lunch menus/plats du jour are better value than dinner)
- Luxury: €300+ — boutique hotels, Michelin-starred dining, private wine tours

## Best Time to Visit
- **Spring (April–June):** Best weather, blooming gardens, fewer crowds than summer. Paris fashion weeks in March/October.
- **Summer (July–August):** Peak season everywhere, especially the Riviera. Many Parisians leave the city in August, making it quieter.
- **Autumn (September–October):** Harvest season in wine regions — excellent for vineyard visits. Comfortable temperatures.
- **Winter (November–March):** Christmas markets in Alsace (Strasbourg) are magical. Ski resorts in the Alps open December–April.

## Visa & Entry
EU/EEA citizens travel freely. Citizens of the USA, Canada, UK, Australia, and many others can visit France (Schengen Area) visa-free for up to 90 days within any 180-day period. An ETIAS travel authorization will be required starting 2025 for previously visa-exempt visitors.

## Cultural Tips
- Greet shopkeepers and restaurant staff with "Bonjour" upon entering — considered basic politeness.
- Lunch (12:00–14:00) is often the main meal; many restaurants close between lunch and dinner service.
- Tipping is not obligatory (service is included) but rounding up or leaving 5–10% is appreciated.
- Pharmacies (green cross sign) are excellent for minor medical issues and will advise on medications.
- Museums are often closed on Mondays or Tuesdays — check before visiting.
